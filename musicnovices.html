Structured musical interactions for novices

For novices playing music together, adding structural constraints leads to better music and may improve user experience

Motivation

While making music with others is enjoyable and rewarding, traditional instruments require years to learn. Simple computer-based instruments allow anyone to play, but but musical novices may still not know what to play or how to achieve musical goals.

A simple instrument

We created an instrument that is easily learnable, regardless of musical training. Using their smartphone, users can control three musical parameters: note density, pitch and timbre. Pairs of users can play duets together. The notes are visible on a shared display, and the instrument is always in key and in time with a musical accompaniment.

Structured interactions

We hypothesized that adding structure would improve users' experience and lead to better music. Structure was created by directing users into different areas of the musical parameter space. Users are instucted to play within blue squares, thus constraining pitch and timbre. Note limits constrain the number of notes players can play in each time period.

Experiment

We invited 8 pairs of users to play two duets together. The control group played two unstructured duets, while the experimental group played a structured duet followed by an unstructured duet.

Adding structure leads to better music

We crowdsourced the comparison of 470+ pairs of duets. We ranked the musical quality of recordings of the first duets. Structured duest were musically better than unstructured duets.

Adding structure might improve user experience

We measured the participant's experience with a post experiment survey. Users in the experimental group listened more to the other player, thought the music was better, and had more enjoyment in the second (unstructured) duet than in the first (structured) duet.
